## 1.1 What is Physical AI?

**Physical AI** refers to the integration of artificial intelligence with physical systems, enabling machines to perceive, reason, and act in the real world. Unlike "Cyber AI" (like LLMs running on servers), Physical AI must deal with the complexities of the physical environment, including gravity, friction, and unpredictable human interactions.

### Key Characteristics:
- **Embodiment**: The AI has a physical body that interacts with the world.
- **Real-time Interaction**: Decisions must be made in milliseconds to maintain balance or avoid collisions.
- **Multimodal Perception**: Using cameras, LIDAR, IMUs, and tactile sensors to understand the surroundings.

---

## 1.2 Anatomy of a Humanoid Robot

Humanoid robots are designed to mimic the human form. This design choice is not just for aesthetics; our world is built for humans (stairs, door handles, tools), so humanoid robots are naturally suited for human environments.

### Major Components:
1. **Actuators**: The "muscles" of the robot (electric motors, hydraulic cylinders).
2. **Sensors**: The "senses" (Cameras for vision, IMUs for balance, Encoders for joint position).
3. **Compute Unit**: The "brain" (GPUs/CPUs for processing AI models and control loops).
4. **Power Source**: Typically high-density lithium batteries.

---

## 1.3 History and Evolution

The dream of humanoid robots dates back centuries, from Leonardo da Vinci's mechanical knight to the modern-day wonders like Boston Dynamics' Atlas and Tesla's Optimus.

- **Early Automata**: Simple mechanical devices.
- **ASIMO (Honda)**: A breakthrough in dynamic walking and balance.
- **The Modern Era**: The shift from programmed movements to AI-driven, end-to-end learning.

---

## 1.4 Current Challenges

Despite rapid progress, several challenges remain:
- **Energy Efficiency**: Keeping a humanoid running for more than a few hours.
- **Generalization**: Moving from a lab environment to a cluttered kitchen or a construction site.
- **Safety**: Ensuring robots can work alongside humans without risk of injury.
